About the Honeybee

Scientific Name: Apis mellifera
Type: Invertebrate
Diet: Herbivore
Group Name: Colony, swarm
Average Lifespan: Up to 5 years
Size: 0.4 to 0.6 inches (workers)

Inside the Hive

Honeybees live in highly organized societies called colonies.
Bee colonies are known as superorganisms because no single bee can survive on its own.
Each bee must carry out its own duties and cooperate with one another to survive.

A bee colony consists of three main roles:

Queen Bee

  • As the only fertile female in the colony, the Queen bee has one role: reproduction.
  • Each queen bee has a unique perfume called a pheromone that only the bees in the hive can smell. This pheromone regulates the behavior of the other bees in the hive.
  • If the queen bee fails to perform at a certain level, the hive will replace her by feeding royal jelly to the youngest developing larva.

Workers

  • Worker bees make up the largest population within a bee colony.
  • Worker bees jobs include housekeeping, feeding other bees and larvae, collecting the pollen and nectar, and making the wax. Their roles depend on how old they are.
  • Worker bees use their barbed stingers to protect the colony. After attacking, the barbs attach to the victim’s skin, tearing the stinging bee’s abdomen, resulting in death.

Drones

  • Drones are hatched from unfertilized eggs and therefore only have one set of genes.
  • Drones only purpose is to mate with queen bees outside their hive. Once they do so, the drone dies.
  • In the fall or winter when food supply is limited, worker bees will prevent drones from entering the hive, leaving them to die.

The Buzz on HoneyBees

A World Without Bees

Bees and other pollinating insects play an essential role in ecosystems.

It's estimated that there are about 2.7 million bee colonies in the U.S. today.

One third of the world’s food supply depends on insect pollination, mainly bees.

There are a variety of insect pollinators, but bees are responsible for the vast majority of pollination.

Honey bees add an estimated $15 billion to the U.S. economy each year in increased crop yields.
