About the Honeybee

Scientific Name: Apis mellifera
Type: Invertebrate
Diet: Herbivore
Group Name: Colony, swarm
Average Lifespan: Up to 5 years
Size: 0.4 to 0.6 inches (workers)

Inside the Hive

Honeybees live in highly organized societies called colonies.
Bee colonies are known as superorganisms because no single bee can survive on its own.
Each bee must carry out its own duties and cooperate with one another to survive.

A bee colony consists of three main roles:

Queen Bee

  • As the only fertile female in the colony, the Queen bee has one role: reproduction.
  • Each queen bee has a unique perfume called a pheromone that only the bees in the hive can smell. This pheromone regulates the behavior of the other bees in the hive.
  • If the queen bee fails to perform at a certain level, the hive will replace her by feeding royal jelly to the youngest developing larva.

Workers

  • Worker bees make up the largest population within a bee colony.
  • Worker bees jobs include housekeeping, feeding other bees and larvae, collecting the pollen and nectar, and making the wax. Their roles depend on how old they are.
  • Worker bees use their barbed stingers to protect the colony. After attacking, the barbs attach to the victim’s skin, tearing the stinging bee’s abdomen, resulting in death.

Drones

  • Drones are hatched from unfertilized eggs and therefore only have one set of genes.
  • Drones only purpose is to mate with queen bees outside their hive. Once they do so, the drone dies.
  • In the fall or winter when food supply is limited, worker bees will prevent drones from entering the hive, leaving them to die.

A World Without Bees

Bees and other pollinating insects play an essential role in ecosystems.

A world without pollinators would be devastating for food production.

Citrus Fruits

Our daily cup of OJ would be a lot more expensive without honeybees.

Blueberries

This superfruit is almost entirely dependent on honeybee pollination.

Coffee Beans

Honeybees are pollination boosters, increasing coffee production up to 25%.

It's estimated that there are about 2.7 million bee colonies in the U.S. today.

Herbs

Herbs like basil, parsley, nutmeg, and dill are largely pollinated by bees.

Oils

Sunflower, canola, and sesame are all dependent on honeybee pollination.

Honey comes in different colors and flavours. The flower where the nectar was gathered from determines the flavour and colour of the honey

Onions

Heavily dependent on honeybee pollination. You'll be shedding tears even when they're gone!

Cherries

Honeybees are crucial in producing enough fruit for a commercially viable crop.

One third of the world’s food supply depends on insect pollination, mainly bees.

Almonds

California's top agricultural export is completely dependent on bees.

Cotton and Flax

Honeybees play an essential role in pollinating important crops.

Bee Products

Bees also create the extremely valuable pollen, beeswax, and propolis.

Cow feed

All the food products in the meat and dairy industry are in danger without bees.

There are a variety of insect pollinators, but bees are responsible for the vast majority of pollination.

Clover and Alfalfa

Crucial feed for cattle, which has huge implications for the meat and dairy industry.

Honey bees add an estimated $15 billion to the U.S. economy each year in increased crop yields.

Protect Honeybees!

Here's what you can do to help!

Plant Flowers

Especially flowers rich in nectar, such as lavender and bluebells! These will help bees find the food they need. It's good to plant flowers in patches too because bees like to focus on one plant type at a time.

Avoid Harmful Pesticides

Synthetic pesticides, fertilizers, and herbicides are harmful to bees. Opt for organic pesticide options if you must, and only spray at night when pollinators are least active.

Create a Bee Bath

Fill a shallow bird bath or a small dish or bowl with clean water, and arrange pebbles and stones inside so that they poke out of the water. Bees will land on the stones and pebbles to drink the water as they take a break from foraging and pollinating.
